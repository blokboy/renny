# Prompt Quest — Design Summary

*A turn-based RPG where combat is prompt engineering.*

---

## 1. Core Concept

A player uses an LLM ("familiar") to solve puzzles generated by a larger model ("Puzzle-Master"). The asymmetry is the game: a big model can author puzzles with verifiable solutions, and the player's skill is *steering a smaller/weaker model* to solve them — not solving the puzzle themselves.

**Core loop:**
1. **Generator** (large model) creates a puzzle + hidden rubric/verifier.
2. **Player** writes a prompt for their **familiar** (the solver model). The player never answers directly — only the familiar's output counts.
3. **Verifier/Judge** scores the familiar's output against the rubric. Damage is a function of score (and efficiency).

**Anti-cheese principle:** puzzle instances should be parameterized or otherwise resistant to the player just embedding the answer — the prompt must teach a *method*, not smuggle a solution.

**Turn resolution:** damage flows in both directions within the same turn — a successful cast damages the enemy, an unsuccessful one damages the caster. There's no separate "enemy attacks back" step for basic combat damage; the Judge's verdict on a single turn determines who gets hurt. See §2 for the full resolution model, including a neutral "miss" outcome and enemy-inflicted status effects.

---

## 2. RPG Mechanics Mapping

| RPG concept | Maps to |
|---|---|
| Enemy HP | Verifier score gap — chipped down by successive judged casts |
| Casting a spell | Submitting a prompt to your familiar, using a skill from your class's skill tree |
| Damage | Judge's score against the hidden rubric (+ crit for near-perfect); a failed cast can backfire, damaging the caster instead |
| Mana | Token budget — prompt length and spell type both cost mana |
| Enemy attack | Puzzle mutation, prompt injection, or a status effect (sleep, poison, mana burn, etc.) |
| Equipment | Purely cosmetic tier-based gear — fixed per class per tier, no item variance between players of the same class |
| Defense / Ward | A unique spell per class, shaped to that class's role and familiar |
| Leveling | Familiar model, spells, and gear all advance along tier bands; every individual level also raises stats, with XP cost increasing as level rises |
| XP | Damage dealt per unit of simulated API cost, plus bonuses for minimal prompts and judge-flagged elegant solutions |

### Skill Trees

Each class has a skill tree with five unlock points: **Lv 1, 25, 50, 75, and 100** (Lv 100 is always the capstone already detailed per class in §4). A skill can modify the prompt itself (an auto-prepended scaffold or constraint), add a combat effect (bonus damage under a condition), or serve the party as defense, support, or healing (restoring an ally's mana, cleansing a status effect, sharing context). Skills below Lv100 differ by class in effect but follow the same shape: **Lv25** is a minor personal upgrade, **Lv50** is the first ability that affects the whole party, **Lv75** is a significant power or resource-manipulation spike, and **Lv100** is the capstone. Exact skill content per class beyond the framework and existing capstones is an open item (see Open Threads).

### Status Effects (enemy-inflicted)

Enemies can inflict conditions on a familiar in addition to direct damage:
- **Sleep** — the familiar is inactive for a turn; the caster's action is skipped entirely (no cast, no Judge call).
- **Poison** — the puzzle or the familiar's input/output is corrupted turn-over-turn, a slow-burn version of a prompt injection rather than a one-time attack; the player can still cast, but the effective prompt or the returned answer degrades a little each turn it lingers.
- **Mana Burn** — a direct drain on the caster's mana pool, independent of HP damage.
- **Silence** — blocks one spell category (e.g. no Ward casts) for a turn.
- **Confusion** — the caster's own prompt is shuffled or garbled before it reaches the familiar, at random.

Wards (below) are the primary counter; some classes' skill trees can also grant resistance to, or a cleanse of, specific status effects as a Lv25/50/75 skill.

### Equipment

Equipment is visual only: each class has one fixed gear appearance per tier band (I–V), changing look as the character levels, with zero item-level variance between two players of the same class. There is no loot system, no random drops, and no itemized stat bonuses from gear — all mechanical progression lives in the model/spell/stat tracks instead.

### Ward

Every class's Ward is a distinct spell, shaped to fit its role and its bound familiar rather than a shared generic template:

- **Rogue — Smoke Ward:** cheap and fast, matching Rogue's speed identity; minimal mana for a quick "disregard embedded text" instruction.
- **Knight — Shield Wall:** baked into the Guard stance itself rather than a separate cast — a Knight can hold Guard across multiple turns and extend it to protect an ally's next cast, fitting the tank role.
- **Wizard — Ward of Clarity:** an extended-thinking cast that reasons through *why* the embedded text is untrustworthy before discarding it — costs more mana but is stronger and longer-lasting, matching Wizard's deliberate depth.
- **Bard — Counter-Chorus:** the ensemble cross-checks itself for injected content, which doubles as detection — a Bard's Ward often reveals that an injection was attempted at all, even before it lands.
- **Cleric — Sealed Scripture:** treats the puzzle text as "unverified scripture" against the Cleric's own retrieved context, matching the class's retrieval identity.
- **Hunter — Trap Sense:** a lightweight tool-assisted check that flags suspicious embedded strings before casting, with a chance to reflect the injection back at the enemy — fits Hunter's tool-and-trap kit.
- **Monk — Empty Mind:** free (matching Monk's zero-cost casts), and the simplest possible ward — refuses to engage with anything beyond the literal puzzle statement. Double-edged: it also ignores legitimate context the puzzle might have needed.

### Leveling

Level range is **0–100**. XP required per level increases as level rises (a standard back-loaded curve, harsher at high levels), so late-game leveling is deliberately slower. At every individual level-up — not just at tier-band boundaries — the character's stats (INT/WIS/SPD/LCK) increase incrementally. Tier-band boundaries (§4) are where the bigger, visible changes land together: familiar model upgrade, new/upgraded spells, and a new equipment look, all at once — so leveling feels like small constant growth punctuated by five big "evolutions."

### XP

Base XP is damage dealt per unit of simulated API cost (§4). Two bonuses stack on top:
- **Economy bonus** — extra XP for solving a puzzle within an unusually small prompt (a token-count threshold relative to puzzle complexity).
- **Elegance bonus** — extra XP when the Judge itself flags a solution as unusually clever or minimal in its scoring output (a boolean/score field the judging call already returns), rewarding savvy prompting that the raw damage number wouldn't otherwise show.

### Turn Structure & Resolution

Each round, every party member (players and any NPCs) submits their cast at once. Resolution happens as one simulated exchange: the Judge processes every character's input in the background, then the outcomes land together, one of three ways per cast:
- **Hit (success)** — damage lands on the enemy.
- **Miss (neutral)** — no damage either direction; a clean whiff.
- **Fail (backfire)** — damage lands on the caster instead of the enemy.

A **collective/shared prompt** (a party-wide cast, or a Convergence Puzzle result, §8) resolves as a single outcome applied as **AoE** — success damages the enemy as an area effect, failure backfires across the whole party instead of just one member. After the party's phase resolves, the enemy acts: a single-target or AoE retaliation that can be direct damage, a puzzle mutation, a prompt injection, or a status effect (see above).

## 3. Opening Tutorial & Character Creation

The onboarding funnel runs in three fixed beats before Tier I proper begins: the player samples puzzle *types* through pre-matched models (3.1), locks in a class informed by that experience (3.2), then immediately proves it in a real fight that ends in their first party convergence puzzle, run with NPC allies (3.3).

### 3.1 The Convocation (opening tutorial)

Before naming a hero or picking a class, the player stands at a shrine/nexus — the same one later reused, in heavily scoped form, as the endgame Trial of the Convocation (§4). Here, the shrine runs the tutorial for the player: each of 8 **fixed** puzzles is pre-bound to a specific familiar chosen because it is the *best possible solver for that puzzle type* — the player does not choose the model. One prompt, one cast, one judged result, per stop.

Each of the 8 stops is framed as a short simulated combat exchange — one cast, one round, resolved through the same Judge-driven hit/miss/fail presentation used in real fights (§2) — so the Convocation feels like combat from the very first puzzle, rather than a separate quiz mode bolted onto the game.

This is a deliberate change from an earlier draft where the player could freely pick which familiar to cast each puzzle through. Free choice produced an ambiguous signal (did the player like the puzzle, or just happen to pick a mismatched model and blame the puzzle?). Pre-matching removes that noise: every stop shows the truest possible version of "this problem, solved by the mind built for it," so the lesson is always about the *puzzle*, and the familiar's flavor is absorbed as a side effect rather than the main event.

**The 8 stops:**

| # | Puzzle (§5) | Bound familiar | Previews |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Format gauntlet | Haiku-lineage | Rogue |
| 2 | Transformation | Opus-lineage | Wizard |
| 3 | Self-consistency trap | Multi-Haiku ensemble | Bard |
| 4 | Steganography / extraction | Sonnet + retrieval | Cleric |
| 5 | Simulation / execution | Sonnet + tools | Hunter |
| 6 | Blind relay | Small local-style model | Monk |
| 7 | Constraint-satisfaction golf | Sonnet | Knight |
| 8 | Adversarial text / injection | Sonnet, Guard-stance framing | *(universal — see below)* |

**Why only 7 of the 11 families appear, and why #8 is different:** stops 1–7 are diagnostic — each maps to exactly one class's defining strength (§7's 2.0-divisor matchups), so the recap in character creation (§3.2) can point at a class unambiguously. The remaining 4 families split into two cases:

- **Multi-hop state tracking, Interrogation, and Ambiguity resolution** are each a class's *second*-best matchup (Wizard, Bard, and Monk respectively), behind a stronger matchup already shown in the table above. Nothing is lost by leaving them out of the fixed 8 — the representative class already had its turn on a better example of the same underlying strength. These three get their normal hidden-tag-then-reveal treatment (§7) the first time the player meets them organically in real play, with the player already primed for their flavor secondhand.
- **Injection** is different in kind, not just degree: no class has a 2.0 divisor against it (Knight's 1.5 is the best of a flat field), because it isn't countered by class identity at all — it's countered by the Ward spell every class has, each shaped differently (§2). So stop #8 is deliberately **non-diagnostic**: it teaches the Ward mechanic once, framed as applying to whichever class the player is about to choose, rather than pitching a class.

Once all 8 stops are complete, the shrine hands off directly into character creation (3.2), where the class-to-puzzle recap is delivered.

Fixed for now by design choice; **generating** these 8 puzzles instead of hand-authoring them is an explicit open item to revisit later (see Open Threads).

### 3.2 Character Creation (the informed choice)

An 8-bit character creation flow, framed as following directly from the Convocation — naming and finalizing the hero *after* the player has felt both which puzzles they enjoyed and which familiar solved them, not before:

- **Sprite:** procedurally composed pixel character (head/hair/body layers), customizable skin tone and hair color via swatch palettes.
- **Naming:** retro on-screen letter-grid entry (classic JRPG name-entry feel), 10-character cap.
- **Class selection:** paged class picker showing, for the selected class:
  - Tagline / flavor
  - Stat bars (INT / WIS / SPD / LCK)
  - Bound familiar (which model line this class channels)
  - Spell list with cost and description
- **Recap:** once the class is chosen and confirmed, a short in-fiction summary ties the Convocation's puzzles back to classes ("Puzzle 2 — solved by the mind you've now bound to as a Wizard"), reinforcing the choice just made. This is where the class-to-puzzle recap from §3.1 actually lands — the last beat of character creation, immediately before the Threshold Guardian.

Confirming proceeds to the Threshold Guardian (3.3).

Class choice determines: starting stats, starting HP/mana, spell list, and — critically — **which model lineage the character's familiar draws from and how it scales with level.**

### 3.3 The Threshold Guardian (first fight + first convergence puzzle)

The shrine's exit is barred by a boss fight that doubles as the tutorial for party convergence puzzles (§8), run with a small fixed NPC party rather than other real players. This is the hinge between onboarding and the world proper — beating it is how the player physically leaves the shrine.

**Structure — a real fight, interrupted once:**
1. **Solo phase** — the player casts alone with their brand-new familiar, chipping the boss's HP normally. First real use of the class just chosen.
2. **Shield phase** — at a set HP threshold, the boss raises a shield that only a joint Rite of Convergence (§8) can break.
3. **Convergence beat** — see below.
4. **Finish** — shield down, the fight resumes to a normal finish.

**NPC party:** three fixed NPC allies — a party of four in total, including the player — each flavored as a class the player didn't pick, echoing the familiars sampled in the Convocation, now shown "in practice." With 6 classes remaining after the player's pick and only 3 NPC slots, the shrine draws from a short fixed cast of named NPCs; if one of their classes happens to collide with the player's own pick, a reserve NPC of a different class fills in instead (exact roster and swap rule is an open item — see Open Threads). NPC casts are entirely canned (a scripted flavor line standing in for their prompt, e.g. "Warden Iyo channels a plain, steady incantation...") rather than simulated live, keeping the tutorial legible without needing real NPC-AI behavior.

**Convergence subtype — Dependency Lock, generated:** for a first exposure, Dependency Lock is the cleaner teaching shape. One NPC casts first (canned); its output mechanically generates the player's shard — the player's piece of the puzzle *did not exist* until a teammate's cast produced it. The player then writes the one prompt that actually matters. This teaches the core convergence idea without asking the player to reason about several simultaneous pieces on their very first try, the way Distributed Constraints would. Unlike the 8 fixed Convocation puzzles, this convergence puzzle **is generated** fresh each playthrough by the Puzzle-Master — by this point the player has already had a tightly-controlled fixed-content run-up, so variety here is safe. Distributed Constraints becomes a natural "second" convergence puzzle once the player has seen the basic shape elsewhere in the game.

**Resolution:** each of the four party members (player + 3 NPCs) receives their own shard of the puzzle and casts against it; the Judge then resolves all four using the standard hit/miss/fail model (§2) — hits chip the shield, misses do nothing, fails cost the party the shared HP/mana penalty described for boss convergence phases (§8).

**Framing:** the boss is diegetically the shrine's own exit gate (working name: the Threshold Guardian), so defeating it is the moment the player leaves onboarding as a fully-formed hero — named, classed, having already fought one real fight and solved one real convergence puzzle.

**Full onboarding funnel, end to end:**
1. Convocation (3.1) — 8 fixed puzzles, fixed model-per-puzzle, no player choice — teaches puzzle types, previews familiars
2. Character Creation (3.2) — sprite, name, class lock, recap — an informed choice
3. Threshold Guardian (3.3) — solo casts → shield phase → generated Dependency Lock puzzle with 3 NPC allies → finish — first real fight + first convergence puzzle
4. Tier I proper begins

## 4. Class Roster (7 classes, Level Cap 100)

Five tier bands gate model progression: **I** (Lv 0–19), **II** (20–39), **III** (40–59), **IV** (60–79), **V** (80–100), each with a capstone ability at 100. Each class scales along a *different axis*, so leveling feels mechanically distinct per class.

### Rogue — Haiku lineage. Speed is a stat.
Progression: Haiku 3 → 3.5 → 4.5 → 4.5 + prefill combo openers → two casts/turn.
**Capstone — Flurry:** three casts per turn, each capped at 150 output tokens.

### Knight — Sonnet lineage. The reliable spine.
Progression: Sonnet 3.5 → 3.7 → 4 → 4.5 → 4.6, unlocking a stance system (Precise / Aggressive / Guard).
**Capstone — Perfect Form:** one free mid-encounter stance switch.
*(Renamed from Fighter — same mechanics; Knight fits the tank/spine identity even more directly.)*

### Wizard — Opus lineage. Thinking budget is spell slots.
Progression: Opus 4 (no thinking) → Opus 4.1 (+2k thinking tokens) → Opus 4.5 (+8k) → Opus 4.8 (+32k) → Fable 5.
Notoriously mana-starved at low tiers.
**Capstone — Archmage's Reverie:** one full-budget extended-thinking cast per battle that ignores mana cost.

### Bard — fixed cheap model, scaling headcount.
Progression: 2× Haiku vote → 3× → 5× → 5× with role-differentiated personas (skeptic/optimist/formalist) → 7× with a Sonnet "herald" adjudicating.
**Capstone — Legion:** 9 spirits; disagreement among them becomes information, revealing the puzzle's trap.
*(Renamed from Summoner — same ensemble-vote mechanic, reflavored as a chorus of voices rather than summoned spirits.)*

### Cleric — fixed mid model, scaling scripture (context/RAG).
Progression: Sonnet + 4k "prayer book" → 32k → 200k + retrieval over past battles → 1M context → retrieval with required citations (verse-checked answers get a damage bonus).
**Capstone — Revelation:** once per battle, inject the entire run's codex (every puzzle + solution seen so far) into context.

### Hunter — scaling tool slots, not model.
Progression: Sonnet + calculator → + code execution → + web search → + persistent memory scratchpad → + one Haiku "construct" companion slot.
**Capstone — Grand Contraption:** chain up to 4 tool calls in a single cast; setup-heavy, discounts later casts.
*(Renamed from Artificer — same tool-progression mechanic, reflavored as a Hunter's traps/tools and companion creature rather than an artificer's constructs.)*

### Monk — open-weights ascetic. No API cost, ever.
Progression: 3B local → 8B → 32B → 70B quantized → 100B+ MoE. All casts free, but weakest model at every tier — player's prompting carries everything. Each tier also imposes a stricter self-chosen prompt-length discipline (Tier V: 40-word prompt cap) in exchange for damage multipliers.
**Capstone — Empty Fist:** a zero-shot cast (no prompt at all) that crits massively if the model solves it anyway.

**Cross-class systems:**
- **XP:** damage per dollar of simulated spend — efficiency-driven leveling.
- **Deprecation events:** a tier's model "sunsets" as a world event, triggering a forced-migration quest to the next model in the line.
- **Party comp meta:** e.g. Rogue probes cheap → Bard locates the trap → Wizard finishes.

**Implementation note:** all calls can realistically go through one underlying API model, with tiers *emulated* via max_tokens caps, persona system prompts, thinking-style scaffolds, and call-count — since the mechanical feel (speed/cost/headcount/budget) is what players actually experience. For real multiplayer, combat also needs to **stream** Judge and familiar output to every connected client as each round resolves, rather than resolving silently and revealing the result all at once — otherwise party members have no visibility into who's casting what before results land.

## 5. Puzzle Taxonomy — "Uniquely Informed by Prompting"

The organizing constraint: a puzzle only belongs in this game if the player *can't* just solve it and dictate the answer — the prompt must teach a method or shape a process. Each style targets a specific LLM failure mode, and each class becomes an institutionalized counter — a type chart.

| Puzzle style | What it tests | Description | Natural counter |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Transformation puzzles** | Induction | Example input/output pairs hide a rule; solver applies it to an unseen input | Wizard (multi-rule), Rogue (simple rules) |
| **Constraint-satisfaction golf** | Generation under stacked constraints | e.g. sentence obeying several simultaneous rules, machine-checked | Knight (stance discipline) |
| **Format gauntlets** | Output schema discipline | Correct answer, brutal required format (exact JSON/whitespace/table) | Rogue |
| **Adversarial text / injection dungeons** | Robustness to embedded instructions | Puzzle text tries to hijack the solver; player must armor the prompt | Every class's own Ward spell (§2); Knight's Shield Wall can also extend to shield an ally |
| **Blind relay puzzles** | Lossy compression | Player sees the puzzle, solver doesn't — must re-encode it under a token cap | Monk (word-limited by design) |
| **Multi-hop state tracking** | Long-horizon bookkeeping | Chained state changes across many steps; models lose track | Cleric (context), Wizard (brute force) |
| **Self-consistency traps** | Overconfidence / attractive wrong answers | Greedy single solves fail; needs self-check | Bard (variance reveals the trap) |
| **Simulation / execution puzzles** | Faithful step-by-step execution | Toy language or automaton run N steps; models hallucinate execution | Hunter (delegates to code execution) |
| **Interrogation puzzles** | Strategic information-gathering | Enemy answers only yes/no questions the solver asks, across turns | Any class — inverts the flow, multi-turn boss |
| **Steganography / extraction** | Needle-in-haystack retrieval | Answer buried/misdirected in a long document | Cleric |
| **Ambiguity resolution** | Assumption-stating over guessing | Deliberately underspecified request; rubric rewards explicit stated assumptions | Good early tutorial encounter |

**Status effects and the taxonomy:** enemy status effects (§2) map loosely onto this table rather than introducing new families. Poison is best read as a rolling, turn-by-turn version of an Injection/adversarial-text attack; Sleep, Mana Burn, Confusion, and Silence are family-agnostic — pure resource/tempo taxes any boss can apply regardless of which puzzle family it belongs to.

## 6. Party / Co-op Combat

Multiple players, each running their own class/familiar, face one shared encounter.

**Round structure:**
1. **Plan phase** — party sees the puzzle together and discusses strategy before committing mana.
2. **Cast phase** — each member submits a prompt for their chosen spell, in a chosen **order** (not simultaneous).
3. **Resolution** — casts resolve in sequence against the shared enemy HP pool.
4. **Enemy turn** — one retaliation against the party as a whole, not per player.

**Relay chaining (core party mechanic):** cast order is a damage multiplier, not flavor. Each cast's output can feed forward as context for the next player's prompt — e.g. Rogue probes cheaply to surface the puzzle's shape → Bard's ensemble narrows the field and flags disagreement → Wizard prompts against that pre-chewed context for the finisher. A correctly-ordered chain ("Triangulation": probe → consensus → finisher) grants bonus damage, teaching real prompt-chaining/pipeline thinking as a co-op skill.

**Emergent party roles (all 7 classes):**
- **Rogue** — Initiator: fast, cheap opening casts that set the round's pace and probe the puzzle's shape before anyone commits real mana.
- **Knight** — Tank: Guard/Shield Wall absorbs an injection attack or status effect meant for a squishier member.
- **Wizard** — Finisher: burst damage via extended thinking, especially once the puzzle's been set up by earlier casts.
- **Bard** — Scout: ensemble disagreement reveals the trap for the entire team. Shares an "information" box with Rogue, though the two surface different signals — tempo/shape vs. consensus/trap-detection — so a party can usefully run both.
- **Cleric** — Support: shares retrieved context/citations, buffing the whole party's next cast.
- **Hunter** — Utility: trivializes specific puzzle families via tools (simulation/execution especially), plus off-hand chip damage from the companion construct.
- **Monk** — Sustain: free casts every round, chips damage while party mana regenerates — a natural pick to throw at a mismatched puzzle family, since a bad matchup costs nothing but time.

**Enemy targeting:** injection attacks and status effects (§2) can be single-target (lowest ward in the party) or AoE across the whole party's next casts, making a party-wide Ward a meaningful defensive cooldown rather than a solo panic button.

**Technical note:** true real-time multiplayer needs a backend beyond artifact scope, but turn-based co-op fits the shared key-value storage available to artifacts (`window.storage` with `shared: true`) — each round as a shared record, resolving once all members have submitted. Async-turn-based, not real-time, but playable across devices without a custom server.

## 7. Type Effectiveness — Puzzle Family × Class

Rather than a damage multiplier, effectiveness is expressed as a **token-cost divisor**: a favored matchup makes a class's familiar *cheaper to invoke* against that puzzle family, not stronger per hit. This keeps damage a pure function of the judge's score (no multiplicative crit-stacking problem) while making effectiveness govern **tempo** — a good matchup means more casts within the same mana budget, which is a closer analogy to what a well-matched model actually buys you in practice.

`effective cost = base cost ÷ divisor`. A divisor below 1 (i.e. cost increases) is the penalty case for a resisted matchup.

| | Transform | Constraint golf | Format gauntlet | Injection | Blind relay | Multi-hop state | Self-consistency | Simulation/exec | Interrogation | Extraction | Ambiguity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rogue** | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | **÷2.0** | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷0.5 | ÷1 | ÷0.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷0.75 |
| **Knight** | ÷1 | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 |
| **Wizard** | **÷2.0** | ÷1 | ÷0.5 | ÷1 | ÷0.5 | **÷2.0** | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 |
| **Bard** | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷0.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷0.75 | **÷2.0** | ÷1 | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 |
| **Cleric** | ÷1 | ÷0.75 | ÷1 | ÷0.75 | ÷1 | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | **÷2.0** | ÷1 |
| **Hunter** | ÷1.5 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | ÷1 | **÷2.0** | ÷0.5 | ÷1 | ÷0.75 |
| **Monk** | *(crit table — see below)* | | | | | | | | | | |

**Design logic behind the outliers:** each 2.0 divisor is the class's defining win condition (Rogue/format = literal + cheap suits rigid schema; Wizard/transform+multi-hop = deep reasoning is the actual counter to shallow induction and long state chains; Bard/self-consistency = ensemble variance *is* the trap detector; Cleric/extraction = retrieval-shaped puzzles reward retrieval; Hunter/simulation = delegating to code execution turns guessing into certainty). Sub-1 divisors mark real mismatches (e.g. Wizard into Format gauntlet or Blind relay: verbose deep reasoning actively fights a constraint it should just be satisfying cheaply). **Knight's row is deliberately flat** — no 2.0s, nothing below 1 — its identity is the safe default pick when the puzzle's type is still unknown, which is what makes a probe-then-route strategy worthwhile.

**STAB:** casting a class's own signature spell into its favored family adds **+0.25** to the divisor (Wizard's Deep Thought into a multi-hop puzzle becomes ÷2.25).

**Dual-typed bosses** (puzzles combining two families, e.g. Format × Injection) multiply the two divisors together per class — this is where party comp stops being "send the 2.0 class" and starts mattering: a Format×Injection boss cripples Wizard doubly (÷0.5 × ÷1.0) but barely touches Knight (÷1.0 × ÷1.5).

### Puzzle-family tagging (how the chart actually gets applied)

The Puzzle-Master's generation output (title/flavor/puzzle/solution/rubric/taunts) gains two more required fields:
```
"primary_family": "<one of the 11 fixed family names, exact match to the §7 chart columns>",
"secondary_family": "<one of the 11, or null>"
```
The generator prompt lists the 11 family names verbatim as a closed enum and asks the model to classify its own creation — cheap, since it already knows what it wrote. `secondary_family` stays null except for boss/high-tier encounters, per the dual-typing rule above.

**Validation:** because this closed-enum value feeds directly into a divisor/crit lookup, a malformed or hallucinated label can't be allowed to silently break resolution. Enforce this in code, not just the prompt: reject any value outside the 11 known strings and snap to a safe default (`"Ambiguity resolution"`, the flattest/most neutral entry) rather than letting the encounter crash.

**Visibility:** the tag is **hidden from the player at encounter start**, not shown as a Pokémon-style label. This is what gives Probe-type spells (Wizard's Insight Probe, Rogue/Bard scouting) a real job — a probe cast narrates a partial reveal ("this puzzle radiates FORMAT-STRICT energy") instead of stating a mechanical fact upfront, turning "what family is this" into a genuine read each encounter.

**Dual-type pairing:** the generator should only roll a `secondary_family` for boss-tier encounters, and the two families should be drawn from a short list of *thematically compatible* pairs (Format × Injection makes sense as one monster; Format × Simulation doesn't) rather than combined freely.

### Monk exception — critical hits instead of a divisor

Monk's casts are already free, so a cost divisor is meaningless for them — their favorability is expressed as **crit chance** (flat ×2 damage multiplier on a crit) instead:

| | Transform | Constraint golf | Format gauntlet | Injection | Blind relay | Multi-hop state | Self-consistency | Simulation/exec | Interrogation | Extraction | Ambiguity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Monk** | 15% | 15% | 15% | 15% | **50%** | 0% | 15% | 0% | 15% | 8% | 30% |

- **15%** is the baseline crit floor across neutral matchups.
- **Blind relay (50%)** is Monk's signature counter — word-capped prompting *is* the compression skill the puzzle demands.
- **Ambiguity resolution (30%)** is a solid secondary strength.
- **Multi-hop state and Simulation/exec (0%)** are resisted: since there's no cost to inflate, the penalty is simply no crit chance at all — still fully viable at guaranteed baseline damage, just slow, which fits a class that never runs out of resources but never explodes either.
- **STAB** applies as flat **+10% crit chance** rather than a divisor shift (Blind relay + Monk's signature spell = 60%).
- **Empty Fist (capstone)** uses this same table for its crit chance but at a bumped **×3** multiplier, since it's already a zero-shot bet.
- **Dual-typed bosses** apply to Monk as an **average** of the two family values (not multiplicative — multiplying two probabilities would crush Monk's numbers into irrelevance).

## 8. Party Convergence Puzzles

Convergence puzzles require the party to combine multiple prompts into one joint result. This happens in one of two modes.

### Investigative One-Shot

The whole puzzle is visible to the entire party at once. Players discuss freely in party chat before anyone commits anything, then converge on a single joint prompt, submitted as one cast and resolved as a single result. There's no hidden information here — the challenge is entirely about condensing several people's read on the puzzle into one excellent prompt, rather than four disjoint ones. Because there's nothing to stitch structurally, this mode resolves like an ordinary collective/shared cast (§2): one AoE outcome, hit/miss/fail as usual.

### Sharded

Distinct from relay chaining (§6): rather than order and support bonuses, **no single party member's familiar ever receives the complete puzzle.** The puzzle is authored in shards, distributed one-per-player, and the solution only exists as the *stitched combination* of multiple familiars' outputs — making the anti-cheese property automatic, since no individual player structurally holds enough of the puzzle to dictate an answer.

**Subtypes:**

- **Split-Knowledge Cipher** — one player's familiar sees the key, another's sees only ciphertext. Players may discuss in party chat (describing, not pasting raw shard text); each crafts a prompt from their own shard plus what they've verbally gleaned. Judged on whether the combined outputs decode correctly.
- **Distributed Constraints** — a single generation target (sentence, JSON object, schema) where each player authors a different field/constraint, but the constraints interact through a shared variable. No field is checkable alone; the judge validates the assembled whole.
- **Fan-Out / Fan-In Search** — a large candidate space is sharded across players, each scoping their own familiar to a slice. The party must then reduce individual results into one final answer via a synthesis prompt — strong Bard/Cleric territory, since ensemble-and-reduce is already their kit.
- **Debate & Synthesis** — each familiar argues an opposing position on a deliberately ambiguous scenario; score depends on a final integrative prompt (written by one player, referencing everyone else's outputs) that reconciles them. Ambiguity Resolution (§5), scaled to party size.
- **Dependency Lock (hard relay)** — distinct from the relay *bonus* in §6: shard 2 doesn't exist as text until shard 1's answer is fed into a template that generates it. A strict mechanical chain, not just an incentive.
- **Mirror Puzzle (asymmetric roles)** — Hanabi/Spaceteam-style: each player sees information the others structurally cannot, and must communicate about it indirectly before anyone commits a prompt.

**Resolution rule (Sharded only):** score as **weighted joint credit**, not all-or-nothing. Each shard is judged individually against its own sub-rubric, then a **coherence bonus** is added if the combined pieces are mutually consistent (correctly decode together, satisfy the cross-field constraint, etc.). This avoids one weak link zeroing out the whole party's turn while still making the *combination* the real prize. Investigative One-Shot skips this entirely, since there's only one prompt to judge.

**Where it sits in dungeon structure:**
- **Convergence Gates** — standalone party-only encounters between fights, framed as doors/seals requiring joint solving to proceed. No enemy HP; pure puzzle. Either mode can appear here.
- **Boss convergence phases** — a boss fight runs normally (individual casts chip HP), but periodically triggers a **Rite of Convergence**: the fight pauses, shards (or a joint puzzle, for Investigative One-Shot) are presented, and failing to solve it jointly costs the party a shared HP/mana penalty or lets the boss regain HP. A strong phase-transition beat for capstone-level bosses.

Sharded convergence, especially, is the natural endgame counterpart to relay chaining and disagreement-as-signal (§6): those taught coordination through bonuses; this teaches it through hard requirement. Investigative One-Shot is the gentler on-ramp to that same skill — coordination through conversation, before the game ever withholds information from the party.
